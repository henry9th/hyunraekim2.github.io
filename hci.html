Barvel

A bar app designed by the Exchangers
           

Introduction

Kevin, Helen, Peter, Emil, and myself make up the Exchangers and our first project for the semester was to design an app that would target specific needs of a type of restaurant of our choosing and its customers. Our team decided to tackle the issues that a bar and its customers faces. Some of these issues are the difficulty of verbally ordering at a crowded bar, effectively making complicated orders, and waiting time for bars without servers.

Work/Thought Process   |   Demo


Props to Kevin for an awesome video!

Issues   |   Solutions   |   Design Considerations

  • Because the app will be used in a dark setting, we used black backgrounds and other dark colors for app elements.

  • Assuming that most customers will have a drink or two, we implemented large buttons with images for slightly intoxicated customers to navigate through the app. In addition, we increased the default toolbar size.

  • Added a text box for any notes to the bartender along with main customizations so users can express smaller details. important for customeres with allergies or strong preferences.

  • A favorites tab so less hassle to order "the usual"

  • A history tab that not only serves as a receipt but a reminder of how much exactly he or she drank.

  • Push notification so users do not have to wait by the bar for their drinks.

  • Bars are usually pretty trendy so we went for a trendy but simple look for our app.

The Process:   Design   |   Development

Before even beginning to design, the group sat down to brainstorm the purpose of a bar app. We chose a bar simply because it was interesting without considering the different needs for a bar app. Our first meeting was dedicated to coming up with a POV and determining what features our app needed without any designing. The work was divided in terms of features and each member had a different part of the app to develop.

 
Our group, mostly having previous Android experience, chose to use Android Studio to design the app instead of using an app design software. However, being a 5-person project, we faced many issues with project configuration and version control. In addition, we faced many issues when it came to making small design changes, most notably increasing the cart icon on the toolbar. However, our group met up on many occassions for hours at a time to overcome the complications.

On our second meeting, we continued to discuss and answer key questions for the app. What were our target customers? What are the main needs we are fulfilling? What are the different design considerations that need to be made? On our third meeting, we drew out a storyboard of the app. Once again, we divided up the work to develop. Our fourth and final meeting was to address the fact that each of our pages did not have a cohesive theme or styling. We came together and made both style and intuitive changes to each of the pages.

       
Overall, while we did develop outside group meetings, we worked mostly in a group meeting room. This way, we always had more than one pair of eyes on each page, leading to conversation and suggestions for improving the design. Also, dealing with merge conflicts in-person was key.

Finally...

Overall, I think this was one of the most capable and hard-working group I've worked with for a school project. Each member was dedicated to making high-quality work. In addition, the group was always open to meeting and working together. Looking back now, I think we overcame some considerable hurdles simply because we worked together. Thanks to everyone!

I do think using a design software could have cut a lot of time that could be used to build other features and be more ambitious with our designs. Otherwise, I am satisfied with how the prototype and the presentation/video turned out.

MR App: Running

An MR app designed by the ExchangARs

Demo

Introduction

Our team consisting of Kevin, Elise, Adam, Michael, and myself were tasked with developing an MR application that could help promote a type of engagment in any domain. For our project, we decided to target the domains of health and entertainment. Our goal in developing an MR application was to promote a healthier lifestyle by gamifying running. You can see our ideation and development process below.

POVs

We came up with two different POVs to reflect two personas with the main difference of their exercise level.

  • Persona 1

    • Demographics: Student who spends too long sitting in the library
    • Attributes: Finds the gym boring and monotonous, but enjoys outdoor activities
    • Goals: Get healthier, look better, and have fun exercising

  • Persona 2

    • Demographics: Student who spends too long sitting in the library
    • Attributes: Enjoys the gym but doesn’t enjoy cardio as much
    • Goals: Maintain fitness but improve cardiovascular health

Interviews

We conducted interviews with three different HKUST students to get a general idea for their issue with exercise and running specifically. We asked each approximately 10 quesitons regarding their current health routine, whether they are looking to improve the health routine, what stops them from improving their health routine, their thoughts on running, and what make them find running more interesting. Below is a video compilation of key responses.

Brainstorm

We proceeded to brainstorm some ideas of features we were thinking of implementing via mind-map.

 

Storyboards

We created two storyboards for each of the personas to demonstrate how each persona would be interested and could benefit from our ideas.

       

Speed Dating

For the speed dating stage, we brought back our same interviewees and showed them our storyboards along with our idea for the app. The general feedback that we would get is that the idea was good and that they would be interested themselves in using the app. The only repeated criticism we received was regarding how they would run wearing a holokit, but our intended use case would regard the app being integrated with a pair of sports glasses/goggles.

Prototyping

Can't say too much about this portion of the process other than that Kevin and Michael did an awesome job working with Unity.

My Contributions

As a team, we were split into two teams. One for the ideation, empathy, and presentation and the other being the development team. For this project, I took a step back from development and was part of the first team. In addition to everything our team did, I was tasked with compiling together the interview videos and the prototype demo videos.

Finally...

Again, I got to work with a very capable team. Everyone had a role which best suited their strengths. For example, our two developers Kevin and Michael had previous experience with Unity, allowing them to efficiently develop a prototype that aligned with our results from ideation. Despite a busy schedule for each of the members, we were successfully able to finish the project without too much strain on any member, and I think that in itself is a large accomplishment. In terms of the app that we created, I believe that the app would be the extra push for me to go running. However, there are many improvements and features that can be added such as having pre-made routes, difficulty ratings, and other crowdsourcing-based features. Of course, there needs to be improvments on AR technology and also a more seamless integration of the app in the forms of glasses, but overall, the prototype showed a good example of what our aims were.

HRI: Ty the Typhoon Buddy

An interactive robot designed for Pepper

Demo

Introduction

This time around, I had the opportunity to work with Kevin, Helen, Jason, Tiantian to design an interface for Softbank's Pepper. The task at hand was to design a robot that serves assistance either before, during, or after the typhoon. Our team decided to design a robot to be used during the typhoon, due to the potentially interesting functions it can serve. Our decision was to design a robot that can serve as a companion and assistant for people who are stuck in a typhoon by him or herself.

POVs

We came up with two different POVs to portray two types of people who would best benefit from our robot.

  • Jane, 21

    • Bio: College student
    • Situation: Stuck inside college dorm by herself in a single person room. No close friends in the same dorm. There is also a power outage due to the storm.

  • Steven, 12

    • Bio: Middle school student
    • Situation: Parents are stuck at their workplace and Steven is alone at home. Cannot easily communicate with parents and need guidance on what to do to ensure a safe and pleasant typhoon experience.

Interviews

We were able to talk to a few HKUST students who were on campus during typhoon. The insight from exchange students was especially useful due to the fact that many of the exchange students didn't have close friends within the dorm early in the semester. In addition, it was their first typhoon that they've experienced. We asked the simple questions such as what the general experience was like for them, what they did during the typhoon, and what could have improved the experience.

Brainstorm

We organized our thoughts using a mind-map. Focusing on the main situation, we came up with key needs of the user during a typhoon and then we began to think of features that could help serve these needs. We then came up with examples of implementations of these features.

 

Storyboards

We created three storyboards for each of the main features that we wanted to implement to demonstrate the intended use case and functionality. You can see the exact feature on the mindmap for reference.
